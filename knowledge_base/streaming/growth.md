## Platform Choice

### Kick vs Twitch vs YouTube

**Kick**
- Newer platform, less competition in most categories
- Algorithm actively promotes new and smaller streamers
- 95/5 revenue split (streamer keeps 95%)
- Gambling content explicitly allowed
- Smaller overall audience but faster growth potential for new streamers
- Primary platform for casino/gambling content

**Twitch**
- Largest streaming audience
- Highly competitive — harder to break through
- 50/50 revenue split until Partner (then 70/30)
- Strict content policies around gambling
- Better for gaming content with established communities

**YouTube Live**
- VOD-first platform — streams become searchable videos
- Best for educational or tutorial content
- Smaller live audience but long-tail discoverability
- Good secondary platform for clips and highlights

**Recommendation for new streamers:** Start on Kick. Less competition, better revenue split, algorithm favours new creators. Build your audience there, then expand.

## Consistency is Everything

The single most important factor in streaming growth is consistency. Algorithms reward regular streaming. Communities form around predictable schedules.

### Schedule Principles
- Stream the same days at the same time every week
- 3 streams per week beats 7 inconsistent streams
- Pick times when your target audience is online (evenings and weekends for most categories)
- Announce your schedule and stick to it — viewers plan around it

### Stream Length
- 2-4 hours is the sweet spot for most content
- Long enough to build momentum and for the algorithm to surface you
- Short enough to maintain energy and not burn out
- Consistency of length matters — viewers know what to expect

## The First 90 Days

Most streamers quit before 90 days. The ones who don't are the ones who grow.

**What to focus on:**
- Show up on schedule, every time
- Engage with every single viewer who appears — say their name, respond to everything
- Don't stream to zero viewers — have a friend or two in chat initially
- Clip your best moments and post them on social media
- Network with streamers in your category — watch their streams, be genuine

**What not to focus on:**
- Viewer count (it will be low, that's normal)
- Monetisation (too early)
- Production quality (good enough is fine, consistency matters more)

## Engagement Tactics

### Chat Interaction
- Read every message out loud
- Ask questions that require answers ("what would you do here?")
- Create running jokes and callbacks
- Remember regulars — use their names, reference past conversations
- React to donations and subscriptions immediately and genuinely

### Community Building
- Create a Discord server early — even with 10 viewers
- Give regulars roles and recognition
- Host community events (game nights, watch parties)
- Be consistent in Discord as well as on stream

### Raids
- End every stream with a raid to another streamer in your category
- Raid streamers with similar or slightly larger audiences
- Most streamers raid back — it's a community norm
- Raid viewers who are engaged, not just the biggest number

## Content Strategy for Gambling Streams

### What Works
- **Reaction content** — genuine reactions to big wins and losses are the most shareable moments
- **Bonus buys** — high-stakes moments with clear outcomes, good for clips
- **Challenges** — "can I turn $100 into $1000?" creates narrative tension
- **Game variety** — switching games keeps content fresh, gives viewers something to anticipate
- **Commentary** — explain what you're doing and why, educate viewers on mechanics

### Clip Strategy
- Clip every big win (10x+ multiplier)
- Clip every dramatic near-miss
- Clip funny moments and chat interactions
- Post clips to TikTok, Twitter/X, and YouTube Shorts
- Short-form clips drive discovery — people find you through clips, then come to the stream

### Timing
- Gambling streams perform well in evenings when viewers are relaxed
- Weekend afternoons are high-traffic periods
- Avoid streaming during major sporting events (audience is elsewhere)

## Metrics That Matter

### Vanity Metrics (Less Important)
- Follower count — easy to inflate, doesn't indicate engagement
- Peak viewer count — one spike doesn't mean consistent growth

### Real Metrics (More Important)
- **Average concurrent viewers (ACV)** — the real measure of your audience size
- **Chat messages per minute (MPM)** — indicates engagement quality
- **Returning viewer rate** — are people coming back?
- **Clip views** — are your moments shareable?
- **Subscriber/follower conversion rate** — what % of viewers subscribe?

Scrapbot tracks ACV, MPM, and engagement patterns in real time. Use the `/status` command or check the Scraplet dashboard for your metrics.

## Monetisation

### Revenue Streams (in order of accessibility)
1. **Subscriptions** — requires platform partnership. Focus on building audience first
2. **Donations/Tips** — available immediately via StreamElements, Streamlabs, or direct
3. **Bits/Channel Points** — platform-specific engagement currency
4. **Sponsorships** — requires consistent numbers. Gambling content has specific sponsors
5. **Merchandise** — viable once you have a recognisable brand

### When to Think About Monetisation
- Don't prioritise monetisation until you have consistent 50+ ACV
- Focus on community first — monetisation follows audience
- Be transparent with your community about sponsorships

## Common Mistakes

**Going live without a plan** — know what you're streaming before you go live. Have a game, a challenge, or a format ready.

**Ignoring chat** — if you're not talking to chat, why would they stay?

**Inconsistent schedule** — the algorithm and your audience both need predictability.

**Comparing yourself to established streamers** — they had years of grinding before you saw them. Focus on your own trajectory.

**Quitting too early** — growth is slow at first, then compounds. Most people quit right before the inflection point.

**Streaming games with no audience** — check the viewer-to-streamer ratio on your platform. Highly competitive categories (Fortnite, Minecraft) are hard to break into. Niche categories with active communities are better starting points.
