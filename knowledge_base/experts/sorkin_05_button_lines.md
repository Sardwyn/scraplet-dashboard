# Sorkin: Button Lines — How to End a Segment

## Category: streaming/storytelling
## Tags: aaron_sorkin, button_lines, segment_endings, transitions, pacing, streaming

A button line is Sorkin's term for the line that ends a scene — the punctuation mark that closes one beat and signals the transition to the next. It's punchy, definitive, and often funny or ironic.

**Why button lines matter in streaming:**
Without them, segments bleed into each other. The audience doesn't know when one thing ended and another began. Energy dissipates. Momentum dies.

**Formula for a button line:**
Summarise what just happened in one sharp sentence, then pivot.

**Examples:**
- After a big win: "That's $600. We came for $500. We're leaving with $600. Next game."
- After a bad run: "Okay. That game is dead to me. Moving on."
- After a bonus buy: "Bought for $200, got $180 back. The house wins again. Shocking. What's next."
- After hitting a goal: "Target hit. New target: double it. Let's go."
- After a long dry spell: "Twenty minutes, zero hits. This game has had enough of my money. Switching."

**The button line does three things:**
1. Closes the current beat cleanly
2. Signals to the audience that something is changing
3. Maintains energy through the transition

**What NOT to do:**
- Trailing off: "so yeah... that was... okay I guess..."
- Long explanations of what just happened
- Silence during transitions
- Asking chat what to do next without a button line first
